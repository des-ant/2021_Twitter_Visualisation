The Twitter Presidency

How Obama and Trump used Twitter during their presidency

Obama: An Early Adopter

Twitter, founded in 2006, is an online social networking and microblogging service that allows users to post text-based status updates and messages, known as tweets, of up to 280 characters in length (Tankovska, 2021).

During the 2008 United States presidential election, Barack Obama won the presidency in a landslide victory, defeating John McCain by a margin of nearly 200 electoral votes and 8.5 million popular votes (Lutz, 2009).

A major reason for Obama’s victory was his campaign’s online advocacy, allowing the general public to become engaged and empowered volunteers, donors and advocates through social networks, e-mail advocacy, text messaging and online video (Lutz, 2009).

Barack Obama’s usage of social media in his political campaigns including podcasting, Twitter, Myspace, Facebook, and YouTube has drawn comparisons to the adoption of radio, television, MTV, and the Internet in catapulting his presidential campaign to success and has been a topic of scholarly inquiry (Lewin, 2008).

In the 2008 presidential campaign, Barack Obama had more supporters on Facebook, more “friends” on Myspace, and more “followers” on Twitter than his opponent John McCain - Obama was the most followed person on Twitter (Brandon, 2008; Shade, 2015). At the time, Twitter seemed like an afterthought even though it created the most personal connection among voters (Brandon, 2008).

Obama’s campaign was able to utilise all social media channels more effectively than McCain’s. The campaign also prioritised and tracked prospective voters entirely through to the polls (Krempasky, as cited in Luck, Beaton & Moffatt, 2010). From the table below, it is evident that Obama had a much larger support base than McCain, allowing him to interact with a wider audience, and for voters to advocate the campaign with personal contributions to online networks and communities (Luck, Beaton & Moffatt, 2010).

In March 2021, Barack Obama was the most followed person on Twitter, with nearly 130 million followers, ahead of second-ranked singer Justin Bieber who had 114 million followers (Tankovska, 2021).

Enter Trump

Trump announced his presidential campaign in 2015 with 2.98 million Twitter followers; his follower count increased rapidly thereafter (Lerman, 2020). Like Obama, Twitter has been credited as an important tool in the victory of Trump’s 2016 presidential election campaign (Ingram, 2016).

While Barack Obama was the first US president to fully harness the power of modern social media platforms, including Facebook and Twitter, no American president has used social media - specifically, Twitter - like Donald Trump (Vazquez, Hickey, Krishnakumar & Boschma, 2020).

Trump has tweeted more with each additional year in office (Vazquez et al., 2020). Since taking office in January 2017, he has tweeted more than 25,000 times - an average of 18 times per day, including retweets (Vazquez et al., 2020). His longest break from Twitter in this period was 1.9 days in June 2017, when former FBI Director James Comey was testifying before the Senate Intelligence Committee during the Russia investigation (Vazquez et al., 2020).

White house press secretary Sean Spicer stated that Trump’s tweets were official statements (Landers, 2017).

"The President is the President of the United States, so they're considered official statements by the President of the United States" - Sean Spider

In accordance with “public interest”, Trumps Twitter account remained unmoderated for most of his presidency ("About public-interest exceptions on Twitter", n.d.; Robertson, 2020).

Insults and nicknames

From the start of his term until May 2019, Trump had insulted 598 people, places, and things on Twitter (Lee & Quealy, 2019). Trump also used nicknames to refer to media figures, politicians, and foreign leaders (Bennett, 2018).

COVID-19 Misinformation

On the 5th of October, 2020, Trump tweeted that he would be leaving Walter Reed Army Medical Center, 3 days after his admission after testing positive for COVID-19 (Kolata & Rabin, 2020).

Donald J. Trump

@realDonaldTrump

I will be leaving the great Walter Reed Medical Center today at 6:30 P.M. Feeling really good! Don’t be afraid of Covid. Don’t let it dominate your life. We have developed, under the Trump Administration, some really great drugs & knowledge. I feel better than I did 20 years ago!
6:37 PM - Oct 5, 2020

In Twitter and Facebook posts the next morning, Trump falsely claimed that the seasonal flu was more lethal than COVID-19 (Greenberg, 2020; O'Sullivan, 2020). Facebook deleted the post, while Twitter hid the same message behind a warning about "spreading misleading and potentially harmful information" (Spring, 2020).

Donald J. Trump

@realDonaldTrump

Flu season is coming up! Many people every year, sometimes over 100,000, and despite the Vaccine, die from the Flu. Are we going to close down our Country? No, we have learned to live with it, just like we are learning to live with Covid, in most populations far less lethal!!!
12:03 PM - Oct 6, 2020

Several hours later, Trump reacted to the censorship by referencing Section 230, the section of U.S. law that provides technology companies immunity from liability for moderation decisions (O'Sullivan, 2020).

Donald J. Trump

@realDonaldTrump

REPEAL SECTION 230!!!
4:08 PM - Oct 6, 2020

Trump’s tweets undermined public health messaging, encouraging followers to disregard recommendations to prevent the spread of COVID-19 (Kolata & Rabin, 2020). Scientific, medical, public health, and ethical experts, pandemic survivors, and the families of those killed by COVID-19 expressed horror and dismay at Trump's attempt to downplay the COVID-19 pandemic in the United States (Kolata & Rabin, 2020).

At the time of Trump's tweet, COVID-19 had killed at least 210,000 Americans and was estimated to be 10 times more deadly than the seasonal flu which caused an average of 36,000 deaths per year (Greenberg, 2020; Kolata & Rabin, 2020).

Riots and Twitter Ban

After his election loss, Trump had persistently undermined the election results in the weeks leading to Joe Biden's inauguration.

Donald J. Trump

@realDonaldTrump

Pleased to announce that @KLoeffler & @sendavidperdue have just joined our great #StopTheSteal group of Senators. They will fight the ridiculous Electoral College Certification of Biden. How do you certify numbers that have now proven to be wrong and, in many cases, fraudulent!
2:50 PM - Jan 5, 2021

On January 5, 2021 - the day before Congress assembled to count the electoral votes and formalising Biden’s victory in the presidential election - Trump falsely claimed on Twitter that Vice President Mike Pence had the authority to remove "fraudulent" electoral votes (Liptak, 2021).

Donald J. Trump

@realDonaldTrump

The Vice President has the power to reject fraudulently chosen electors.
4:06 PM - Jan 5, 2021

On January 6, 2021, the United States Capitol in Washington, D.C., was stormed during a riot and violent attack against the U.S. Congress when supporters of President Donald Trump attempted to overturn his defeat in the 2020 presidential election by disrupting the counting of electoral votes (Reeves, Mascaro & Woodward, 2021).

As a result of the riots, five people died, about 140 officers were injured and over $30 million was required for repairs and security measures (Chappell, 2021; McEvoy, 2021; Schmidt & Broadwater, 2021).

Following the U.S. Capitol riot, Trump’s Facebook account was suspended and his Twitter account was permanently banned, leaving behind 88.7 million Twitter followers (Küfner & Martin, 2021; Rattner, 2021).

Similar measures were placed against key allies of Trump, resulting in a 73% decline in the spread of election-related misinformation during the first week following the bans (January 9–15), according to research analytics firm Zignal Labs (Dwoskin & Timberg, 2021).

In Summary

Social media, especially Twitter, can resonate just as strongly with potential voters as traditional media (Morris, 2017). This can account for the success of Barack Obama and Donald Trump’s presidential campaigns (Morris, 2017). However, the exploitation of such platforms can lead to dire consequences.
